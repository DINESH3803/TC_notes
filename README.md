# 📚 Semantics & Semiotics — Exam Study Guide

---

## 1. Core Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Semantics** | The study of how people communicate *meanings* with pieces of language |
| **Semiotics** | The general study of all sign systems (broader than linguistics) |
| **Signification** | The process of creating and interpreting symbols — making one thing stand for another |
| **Sign** | Anything that stands for / represents something else |
| **Signifier** | The form of the sign (e.g., a word, image, sound) |
| **Signified** | The concept/meaning the signifier represents |
| **Idiolect** | An individual speaker's personal version of a language |
| **Metalanguage** | The language used to *describe* another language (the tool of description) |
| **Object language** | The language being described |
| **Pragmatics** | The study of how context and speaker intention contribute to meaning |
| **Mental lexicon** | The internal, mental "dictionary" a speaker holds in their mind |

---

## 2. Key Scholars & Their Theories

### Ferdinand de Saussure (1974)
- Argued that the study of **linguistic meaning** is part of the broader study of **sign systems**
- Introduced the key terms: **signifier** and **signified**
- Stressed that linguistics falls under the larger field of **semiotics**

### C. S. Peirce
- Proposed the foundational **three-way classification of signs**:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Icon** | Sign resembles what it represents (similarity) | Portrait, engine diagram |
| **Index** | Sign is causally/closely associated with its referent | Smoke → Fire |
| **Symbol** | Purely conventional/arbitrary link between sign and signified | Words, military insignia, wearing black for mourning |

> 💡 **Words are verbal symbols** — there is no natural connection between a word and its meaning; the link is purely conventional.

---

## 3. The Definitions Theory

**What it is:** A simple theory of semantics stating that:
- Word meanings = definitions
- Sentence meanings = combination of word definitions (following grammar rules)

**Why it's appealing:** Intuitive and straightforward.

**Why it fails:** It cannot handle three major challenges (see below).

---

## 4. The Three Challenges in Semantics ⚠️

### Challenge 1: Circularity
- **Problem:** Definitions of words are always written in *other words* — the process never ends.
- **Example:** ferret → polecat → domesticated → tame → … (endless loop)
- **Key Question:** Can we ever step *outside* language to describe it?
- **Proposed Solution:** Develop a **semantic metalanguage** — a neutral, bias-free descriptive tool that meets scientific standards (clarity, economy, consistency).
  - Risk: Some argue no perfect metalanguage is attainable; ordinary language may be best.

---

### Challenge 2: Linguistic Knowledge vs. Encyclopedic Knowledge
- **Problem:** Where does word meaning end and general world-knowledge begin?
- **Example:** If you think a *whale* is a fish (wrong) and I know it's a mammal, do we mean different things by "whale"?
  - We still understand each other → meaning seems somewhat independent of full scientific knowledge.
- **Key Question:** How much knowledge is *necessary* to use a word correctly?
- **Proposed Solution:** Design meaning representations (metalanguage) that specify which elements of knowledge count as *semantic* vs. *encyclopedic*.
  - Related debate: psychological theories of **concepts and categories**.

---

### Challenge 3: Context and Meaning
- **Problem:** The meaning of an utterance can change dramatically based on context.
- **Examples:**
  - *"Marvellous weather you have here in Ireland"* → sincere on a sunny day; sarcastic in the rain
  - *"He's dying"* → terminal illness vs. a bad stand-up comedian
  - *"It's getting late"* → could mean *"Let's leave"* at a party
- **Key Question:** If context affects meaning infinitely, how can definitions capture it?
- **Proposed Solution:** Split meaning into:
  1. **Literal/Conventional meaning** — context-free, stable core
  2. **Contextual/Pragmatic meaning** — handled separately by pragmatics
  - Listeners actively use **inference** + **background knowledge** to interpret what speakers *mean* (not just what words say).

---

## 5. Strategies for Meeting the Challenges

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|----------|
| Circularity | Create a **semantic metalanguage** (neutral, scientific) |
| Linguistic vs. encyclopedic knowledge | Model **conceptual knowledge**; argue which elements belong to meaning |
| Context | Separate **literal meaning** from context; study **pragmatics** |

---

## 6. Semantics vs. Pragmatics

| Semantics | Pragmatics |
|-----------|-----------|
| Studies conventional/literal meaning | Studies meaning in context |
| Context-free | Context-dependent |
| What words/sentences mean | What speakers *mean* by using them |

- Listeners are **active**, not passive decoders — they infer speaker intent using contextual clues.
- The relationship and division of labor between semantics and pragmatics is an ongoing debate.

---

## 7. Examples of "Mean" in Everyday Language (from text)

These examples show that "meaning" extends beyond language:

| Example | Type |
|---------|------|
| *Those vultures mean there's a dead animal ahead* | Causal inference (index) |
| *His high temperature may mean he has a virus* | Causal inference (index) |
| *The red flag means it's dangerous to swim* | Arbitrary public symbol (symbol) |
| *Those stripes mean he is a sergeant* | Conventional symbol (symbol) |

---

## 8. Quick-Revision: Key Concepts at a Glance

- **Semiotics > Semantics** — semiotics is broader; semantics is the linguistic subset
- **Saussure** → signifier/signified; language as part of semiotics
- **Peirce** → icon / index / symbol (3-way sign classification)
- **Words = symbols** (conventional, arbitrary link to meaning)
- **Definitions Theory** → simple but flawed (circularity, knowledge boundary, context)
- **3 Challenges** → Circularity | Linguistic vs. Encyclopedic Knowledge | Context
- **Solutions** → Metalanguage | Concept modeling | Pragmatics
- **Idiolect** = individual's personal language
- **Metalanguage** = language used to describe language
